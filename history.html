About Intramuros

Origins and Construction

Intramuros was established in 1571 by Miguel López de Legazpi, the first Spanish governor-general of the Philippines. Its location along the Pasig River and proximity to Manila Bay made it an ideal site for trade, defense, and governance. To protect the city from pirates, foreign invaders, and local uprisings, the Spanish built thick stone walls, bastions, moats, and gates that enclosed the city. Inside these walls, Intramuros was a carefully planned settlement. The streets followed a grid pattern, and the city hosted government offices, churches, schools, convents, and residences of Spanish officials and wealthy families. Its architecture reflected Spanish colonial style, blending both European and local influences.

Restoration

After the war, restoration efforts began to preserve and reconstruct Intramuros’ historical landmarks. Today, it stands as a living museum, attracting tourists and locals who wish to explore Manila’s colonial past. Visitors can walk along cobblestone streets, visit centuries-old churches, museums, and Fort Santiago, and experience the rich history and culture of the Philippines.
